What is gerrymandering?

Gerrymandering is dividing a U.S. state into election districts in a way that gives one political party a majority in the districts while weakening the voting strength of the opposing party by concentrating as many opposing votes into as few districts as possible.

























The term "gerrymandering" was created after the redrawing of Massachusetts district lines. After taking office in 1810, Governor Elbridge Gerry signed a bill to change district lines to benefit his Democratic-Republican party and weaken the Federalists. When mapped, the districts created a bizarre shape that resembled a salamander. The term is actually a combination of the governor's last name and the word "salamander." There is no solid evidence on who authored the term.






























The goal of gerrymandering is to create districts that will win a party more seats in the legislature or protect the ones they currently have. New maps are drawn by the state legislator and then approved by the governor in most states.
























There are 2 main methods of gerrymandering: packing and cracking.

























Packing is when redistricting tries to concentrate as many voters for one party into a single district to decrease its influence in other districts, reducing the number of potential district majorities.







































Cracking is when redistricting tries to break apart a party majority by sectioning the votes into different districts, denying any chance of a winning party majority.
